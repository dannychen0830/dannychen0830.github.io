## On applying to graduate school

I consider myself an graduate school application verteran because I've done it (a whopping) two times. I remember how clueless I was while writing my personal statements, CV, and other miscellanious parts of the application. So, I decided to offer whoever has stumbled on this page another data point for how to tackle the admission process.

### Context 

Context matters. So, I'll put my stats/resume here for reference. 

I graduated from Case Western Reserve University majoring in Math and CS (BAs, though BS options are availabile) (it's considered an R1 institution, but not particularly known for either its math and CS, unlike our biology and BME departments). I had a good GPA (Magna Cum Laude) and have taken a few grad level courses in math, stats, and CS. I started my first research project in the freshmen-to-sophmore summer, resulted in a poster presentation at a conference. My second project was with a math professor working on signal processing, which was in submission for at the time of application. I also had connections with Argonne National Laboratory starting the sophomore-to-junior summer. These resulted in a presentation at the APS march meeting and two preprints at the time of the application. I have never TAed, but was a tutor for 1.5 years. 

I applied to 8 schools (all to the CS department) my first attempt and was rejected from all of them. I applied to 10 the following year (to mainly applied math programs, but had a few exceptions) and has received 5 offers.

I will be comparing my two applications and figure out 1) what makes the first application horrible, and 2) what makes the second application better.

### Prerequisite

Before you apply to graduate school, you should really be asking why. For masters, you'd likely be paying undergraduate-like tuition for another 1-2 years. For PhDs, you won't be paying for the tuition, but you would be making little money for 5 years (this is certainly true if you majored in math, physics, and most engineering---if you're qualified for PhD programs, it is very likely that you're qualified for jobs that pay better than the PhD stipend). The two options are not mutually exclusive. 

You would also want to get to know the field a little bit. You can always switch your resesarch interest, in fact, many do so. Nonetheless, you should have some preliminary goals, problems that you find interesting within the discipline, and some directions that people are heading towards. My advice is start from one professor whose work intrigue you, go on Youtube, and find talks that they gave. The recommendation algorithm would take over from there. If the talks give you an itch of wanting to get to work, then congrats---research is probably a good choice for you. 

### List of schools

The first thing you should do is crafting the list of schools you're applying to. There are two webpages you should be looking at: the research page, the how-to-apply page, and the graduate student page. 

The research page is for finding faculties whose interests align with yours. You should add the school to a preliminary list if there are at least two faculties that you would have no regret working with. And be specific enough in this search. For example, don't say you're interested in AI because the the entire CS department can potentially be interested in some aspect of it. But if you hone it down to "AI and privacy," the list shrinks by quite a bit. 

The how-to-apply page tells you, well, how to apply? In most cases, they would ask for a statement of purpose, 3 reference letters, your most recent transcript, and optionally, test scores and additional statements. The first three are non-negotiable, but the last two you should be aware of. For example, I didn't want to take the GRE, so UPenn and UIUC was off the list despite a match in research interest. UMich and Cornell required extra statements, and I found out pretty much last second and that's no good. Make sure that the extra writings are indeed non-optional and plan ahead. 

Hopefully, the list has narrowed down quite a bit. Depending on how much work you want to put in (and money to spend, application fee can get expensive), you can start to look at the graduate students page. Many graduate students will have a website like this one with their CVs attached. Check when they entered the program, and look at their accomplishments before that. If you find your accomplishments to be similar to most of them in the program, then you can deduce that it is a "target" school. If they're generally much more accomplished, then it is likely a "reach" school. Lastly, if you think you have a more impressive resume than most in the program, it is likely a "safety" school.

Three things to note here: 1) you should have no regret going to any school on your list. If you're hesitant, take it off the list and save some money. 2) The notion of a "safety" school here is really a misnomer. We're talking small chances still. Like all applications, luck is a significant component and there is no way around that. So, have back-up plans. 3) When examining CVs, note that publications can often come after graduation. Give a one-year buffer when trying to determine current grad-students' undergrad accomplishments.

In my first attempt, I crafted a list full of Ivys and top 15s. There wasn't a strong match in interest, and to be quite frankly, I wasn't too passionate about what I proposed in the statement. "Why?" you might ask. Well, I was a bit perplexed with my future direction from all the voices around me. I wanted to stay in engineering and a hot field for better job prospects. And when you're statement of purpose contains many professors in the department you're not applying to, that's a sign that you should reconsider your field of study. 

My second attempt had a good mix of safety and reaches. I also approached the application very differently. I had a great job, so it is not the end of the world if I failed again. I came to terms with what I enjoy, and didn't think of it too much as an economic investment but a personal goal instead. That is, if I graduate in 5 years and return to the same position, I would be content knowing that I have evolved as a mathematician. 

### Cirriculum Vitae or Resume

You want to list out your accomplishments here. Resumes are generally one-page and features a limited number of items with detailed descriptions of each. CVs can be thrown together with less thought. It simply compiles all your achievements---big or small---into a number of pages. As undergrads, CVs are generally not too long; mine was two pages. My personal opinion is that CVs are more appropriate. The missing descriptions in the Cvs can be compensated in the personal statement. CVs are also easier to structure. Find your favorite professor and they usually will have their CVs on their websites. 

Of course, if you have a immaculately formatted resume at your disposal, go right ahead!

### Personal Statement

The personal statement is a tool to tell the admission committee who you are, so just be yourself. There is not a correct way of writing it, so long as it contains: 1) your academic interest, 2) description of your prior experience, and 3) why is the school a right fit. 

A few writing tips for a good personal statement.
1. Have a central theme. This can be a life goal that is achieved through obtaining the degree, or a personal philosophy that motivates you. Mine was building generalizable ideas, which ties together my experiences, interest in interdisciplinary problems, research, and mathematics. 
2. Avoid words that have negative tones. I hate not using double negatives. However, negative-sounding words might do you more harm than good. This also avoids tedious long sentences that fails to articulate your grandiose ideaology. (This bullet point is exactly what you should't do, if you haven't noticed.)
3. Be concise. 
4. If your confident in your writing, try being playful in a few spots. While being professional, of course.
